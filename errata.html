Welcome, fellow traveller. You found the cove of the

Pirates of South Pacific

We are travelling around the islands of Polynesia.

We will be compiling this errata page as we discover things the
Lonely Planet guide to French Polynesia
gets wrong:




1. Electric outlets. According to LP, they are all type A (North American). All that we've seen so far have been type C (European).


2. Cost of travel. There are many ways to travel to French Polynesia that are much cheaper that those presented in the guide. They seem to believe that "budget" travel would be 120 dollars per day per person. (!?) f you want to spend time in Tahiti at about the same cost as modest living in a major city, here's how to hack it. You find accomodations on http://www.petitesannonces.pf/, or, failing that, from an independent host on AirBnB. Booking an AirBnB for a month or longer will often get you much better rates. You cook your own food and take care to buy local products as much as you can - everything that is processed is shipped thousands of miles. Get ready to eat a lot of fruit, veggies and fish. Plus baguettes (every supermarket has them fresh every morning at about half a buck) and locally produced milk. It's pretty great. You'll learn to open coconuts in no time. You can get local produce delivered from https://tahiti-localfarms.com/. Every sunday there are street vendors all around the coast selling the best grilled meat you'll ever eat and lical firi firi donuts. Prices vary. In Puna-Auia we've been paying an equivalent of 5 dollars for a grilled piece of chicken with delicious sauce that's enough to feed us both, and 2 bucks for a bag of firi firi donuts, in Papenoo it was 5 for two fried chicken legs and firi firi altogether. Also, according to the guide, your options to get bikes are a 15-20 bucks a day rentals and that's pretty much it. What they don't mention is that if you're here for longer, buying a bike from Pacific Cycles in Papeete starts below 200 bucks.

I was particularly dissaponted with Lonely Planet on this one, because they are usually very good at catering to budget travellers. Their "on a shoestring" guides are absolute gems, and the brand is pretty much built on backpacker culture.


3. Buses. The cost of bus travel depends on how far you go and is typically an equivalent of 1.5 to 3.5 dollars paid to the driver as you come in. The travel guide says it's a flat fee of 2.5 paid at the end. Anyone else getting the impression it was written by bougie motherfuckers who mostly stick to resorts?


4. Indigenous struggle. The guide pays very little attention to local affairs, and has a very white/francophone bias. The most reference to any Land Defender activity is on page 69 ("Inland Thrills"): "(...) at the time of research the road was closed further down the valley due to a barricade built by the area's residents. Legal proceedings were under way to ensure that the road would remain accessible both to visitors and Tahiti's hydroelectric company workers." CRINGE.


5. None of the cultural references are actually Polynesian. The guide has a standard list of Hollywood movies about Polynesia for you to watch, but no reference to writers or creators that are actually Polynesian. Anyway, I'm just starting to read Chantal Spitz right now cause Wikipedia does a better job of this than Lonely Planet. (Again, LP is usually way better than this.) And as for Hollywood movies, it's pretty on the nose that Moana (not mentioned in the guide, possibly because they both came out in 2016) also did a much better job of understanding Polynesian culture.
